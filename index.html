Self-Assessment

In this section I will try and semi-objectively evaluate my skills in four main dimension of English lanuage (Listening, Reading, Writing, and Speaking) and point out what I believe to be my strong points, as well as the areas I feel still have room for improvement.

Listening

I think my listening comprehension is good -- most of the university courses I attend are taught in English, and virtually all audiovisual media content I consume (such as films or Youtube videos) is also in English. I can mostly understand the words in the songs I'm listening to (depending on the genre, tempo and sound quality -- for example it can be very hard for me to distinguish words in songs like Venom by Eminem). I can easily hold up a natural conversation in English with a native speaker, unless they speak rapidly in an accent uncommonly found on the internet or academia (e.g. Scottish accent).

Another thing that I notice about myself is that when I am watching a film without subtitles, I can sometimes zone out or get my rhythm "interrupted" and completely lose the ability to perceive what the characters are saying for a bit, though it varies depending on pronunciation and sound quality in a film -- for example I didn't need subtitles watching High Noon (1952) but found myself sometimes relying on them watching Trainspotting (1996).

Overall I am confident in my ability to perceive and comprehend audio information, which has increased by a good margin since I've started studying in a university, and I strive to be able to comprehend less clear or complicated (for me) speech in the future.

Reading

I believe my reading comprehension to be on a rather high level -- I can fully understand, grasp, and interpret or reformat complex theoretical ideas and tend to prefer more linguistically and structurally challenging reads. I often work with documentation, research papers, and broader theoretical literature (such as philosophical, theological and anthropological book-length works) -- both in academic setting and as a personal interest.

Some of my favourite books, however, don't necessarily fully align with abovementioned criteria. Here are some of the works that I believe influenced me the most throughout my life: The Sickness unto Death by Søren Kierkegaard, Walk Through Walls: A Memoir by Marina Abramović, Siddhartha, and Steppenwolf by Hermann Hesse, The Stranger by Albert Camus, Humankind by Rutger Bregman, Silent Spring by Rachel Carson, and virtually the entire Kafka's bibliography as a whole.

Of course, sometimes I encounter words or idioms that I don't know the meaning of, and then I look them up in a dictionary. Due to my tastes, I imagine I may have a "blind spot" in my vocabulary when it comes to more technical definitions, such as machinery parts or website elements, or less used ways to refer to animals (for example, it was only recently that I'd learned what a mallard is).

Almost all of the content I consume is in English, reading and looking up any sort of information included, which I believe allows me to actively utilise my English (reading) comprehension skills on a daily basis, as well as helps me build up my vocabulary and other forms of knowledge directly or indirectly related to English -- such as style and format awareness or slang and jargon. I believe my Reading comprehension to be the strongest of the four dimensions, since it's an inherent part of my daily routine and hobbies.

Writing

Writing is a skill I believe to be the hardest to "passively" train, compared to the other three dimensions. Casual daily writing in my life involves mainly text messaging or information search on the internet, which does not automatically make me a better writer -- albeit a faster one. Undoubtedly, I believe my reading habit to have been greatly supporting my writing skills, but properly applying the vocabulary, style and format knowledge is a challenge in itself. I consider my writing proficiency to be at a level expected of a first-year student, which is far from what I actually want it to be. I am able to put in writing whatever ideas and vision I possess, and I am able to make it structurally pleasant. I utilise a broad vocabulary range in my texts and mind the style I am adhering to, and always try to format the text so that it's easy to read, but there are also aspects I tend to struggle with (namely, when it comes to academia).

Since the beginning of my studies, I have undertaken a number of courses that aim to enhance the attendees' writing skills, be it in academic, or artistic style. I have also, as a part of my studies, written a fair share of emails, research papers and assignments, all of which helped me to eventually get better in respective styles. If I had to point out my biggest weakness, it would definitely be academic texts which require citations -- not only have I written rather few of such texts, the citation style required was also non-uniform (some professors required MLA, some APA, etc.), which lead to me still having to look up the proper way to cite when writing papers.

Writing is currently a skill I am putting the most conscious effort in, and I really want it to pay off -- as I've mentioned in the previous section, research papers fascinate me and I want to eventually become good enough to be able to write them mostly unaided.

Speaking

Speaking is definitely among my favourite activities. I am a very sociable and communicative person, and I greatly believe in the power of verbal interpersonal communication. I am not a native of my current country of residence, and while I do speak the local language fluently enough, I will always prefer to communicate in English, which to me sometimes feels even more natural than my native language.

Most of my university courses are taught in English, which means that even outside of them I have the freedom to communicate with my colleagues in English, allowing me to continuously practice my pronunciation and general speaking skill even in the context of everyday life. I have been attending several courses directly or indirectly related to verbal communication, but I believe that Phonetics & Phonology course has been the most beneficial one so far. Apart from vocabulary, I've realised it's also extremely important to pay attention to pronunciation and general clearness of speech.

I can easily hold a natural conversation with a native speaker on a great variety of topics, generally only limited to what I myself am knowledgeable in, since, as I've mentioned earlier, most of the knowledge I possess I obtained in English language. While I am confident in my vocabulary and connectedness of speech, I also have several flaws related to the very process of speaking -- namely, I tend to pronounce sounds like /æ/ and /ɑ:/ in a very similar, nigh indistinguishable manner. Another issue I have and am working on is that when I am talking on a topic I am very fluent in, or retelling a story, I tend to slip into speaking very rapidly, to the point of my words becoming indistinguishable -- something I am also guilty of when speaking in my mother tongue.

While my speaking manner and vocabulary is much more casual and less thought-out when speaking without preparation, I still believe my speaking proficiency to be of a rather high level, both logically and lingustically.

Reading Examples

In this section I will present some of the most challenging reads I've had, evaluate their content and explain why I felt they were difficult.

The Sickness unto Death by Søren Kierkegaard
A cornerstone of Christian existentialism. This book, written in 1849, explores the author's perception of despair and its forms, as well as presents the phenomenon of despair as the very nature of sin. In this work, Søren outlines the three kinds of despair he believes to exist, as well as the cause and the effect thereof.
This book is a rather hard read -- it took me close to two months to complete and understand it. The vocabulary used is not too difficult or technical, but it was the structure and the use of language that proved to pose the biggest challenge -- not only did the translators struggled to find suitable modern English substitutions for old Danish definitions, but the author himself was a terrifying writer, easily comparable to Hegel when it came to incomprehensibility.
Here's one of my favourite quotes from the book:
“The self is a relation which relates itself to its own self, or it is that in the relation that the relation relates itself to its own self; the self is not the relation but that the relation relates itself to its own self.”
Paradise Lost by John Milton
Another classic of Christian literature -- this time in the form of an epic poem. Published in 1667, it explores themes such as Fall of Man, Satan's rebellion, expulsion from Eden, and a number of other classical Christian narratives -- all in the form of a blank verse. It greatly delves into philosophical themes, the most notable one for me was the concept of human freedom within the Christian framework.
It was a pretty hard read, not only because of archaic and very poetic use of language and syntax (often uncharacteristic for English), but also because of countless historical references or biblical allusions which I was not familiar with -- I think I had to look up every other verse of the poem, but overall it was a great learning experience for me.
“Ill worthy I such title should belong
To me transgressor, who for thee ordained
A help, became thy snare: to me reproach
Rather belongs, distrust and all dispraise.”
Beowulf
A classic Old English epic poem of unknown author dating to about 10th century AD. It explores the topics of heroism, humanity, and pride. While the plot itself is not that complex, I still found the Beowulf's psyche and the characters (namely the monsters) well worthy of attention and exploration -- I couldn't shake the feeling that there is way more depth than what meets the eye at first glance -- and the ending itself suddenly put my entire comprehension of the narrative in a different perspective.
The structure of the work was in a way unusual compared to more modern epics I've read, and I noticed a lot of similarities to German syntax structure, which added an additional layer of interest to the read. I didn't read the poem in Old English, but an adaptation written by Lesslie Hall, Ph. D., so I can't say the language used was in itself hard to comprehend, but it was cultural value that made me really delve on the poem and find myself thinking about it even months after having read it.
“O flower of warriors, beware of that trap.
Choose, dear Béowulf, the better part,
eternal rewards. Do not give way to pride.
For a brief while your strength is in bloom
but it fades quickly; and soon there will follow
illness or the sword to lay you low,
or a sudden fire or a surge of water
or jabbing blade or javelin from the air
or repellent age. Your piercing eye
will dim and darken; and death will arrive,
dear warrior, to sweep you away.”
Antigone by Sophocles
Antigone is an Athenian tragedy written in 440 BC and is one of a triad of tragedies known as the three Theban plays. It builds and expands upon the Theban legend and focuses on Antigone's conflict with king Creon -- namely him forbidding anyone to bury Oedipus' body, and Antigone, Oedipus' sister and the princess of Thebes going against the law and burying her brother, for which she is then severely punished. The play mainly explores the king Creon's prideful downfall and the consequence of going against the better judgement in the name of one's own ego.
I read the play in the Penguin Books' translation, which is among the most modern, well-known and easy to comprehend translations available. Despite the play itself being rather short, it still managed to fascinate me and I felt it was a great introduction to Greek theatre plays, and the narrative was very well-structured and engaging, which made me want to read the other two Theban plays.
“All men make mistakes, but a good man yields when he knows his course is wrong, and repairs the evil. The only crime is pride.”
